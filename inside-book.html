Sample Excerpt

From Chapter 1: "The Perfect Life That Wasn't"

I was thirty-five when I realized I had been living someone else's life for so long that I couldn't remember what mine was supposed to look like. The realization hit me like a slap in a quiet restaurant on a Tuesday evening, sitting across from my husband of eight years, watching him scroll through his phone while I tried to remember the last time we'd had a conversation that wasn't about logistics.

From the outside, we were the picture of success. The house in the right neighborhood. The careers that impressed at dinner parties. The social media presence that suggested we had it all figured out. But sitting there, watching the golden light filter through the restaurant windows, I felt more alone than I had ever felt in my life.

The waiter brought our appetizers—something I had ordered because it seemed like the kind of thing a sophisticated woman would choose, not because I actually wanted it. I realized I had been making choices like this for years. Small compromises that felt reasonable in the moment but had accumulated into a life that felt foreign to me.

"How was your day?" I asked, knowing the answer would be fine, busy, the usual. But something in me was desperate for connection, for anything real.

He looked up from his phone, gave me the expected response, then turned his attention back to whatever was more interesting than the woman he had promised to love and cherish. And that's when it hit me: I had spent so much energy trying to be the perfect wife, the perfect employee, the perfect woman, that I had perfected myself right out of existence.

The question that would change everything whispered itself into my consciousness: What would happen if I stopped trying to be perfect and started trying to be real?

Another Glimpse

From Chapter 5: "Learning to Say No (And Mean It)"

The first time I said no without providing a detailed explanation, justification, or alternative solution, I thought the world might end. It didn't. But something in me did die that day—the part of me that believed my worth was contingent on my usefulness to others.

It was a small thing, really. A colleague asking me to take on yet another project that wasn't mine, expecting the usual response: "Of course! I'd be happy to help!" followed by my inevitable spiral into resentment and overwhelm.

But this time, something different happened. My body responded before my people-pleasing brain could kick in. My shoulders tensed. My jaw clenched. My stomach dropped. Every cell in my being was screaming "NO" so loudly I couldn't ignore it.

So I listened. For the first time in decades, I listened to my body instead of my conditioning.

"I won't be able to take that on," I said, my voice steadier than I felt.

The silence that followed felt eternal. I waited for the world to collapse, for relationships to crumble, for proof that I had been right all along—that saying no meant being selfish, difficult, unlikeable. Instead, my colleague simply said, "Okay, thanks for letting me know," and moved on to find another solution.
